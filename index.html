Science that isn't science

I wanted to kick off this inaugural post with a topic I first read about years ago: cargo cult science. A quick Google search turns up Richard Feynman's 1974 CalTech commencement address as the first usage of this term and if you have not read the adaptation, I highly recommend you do so now. Although Feynman was talking from the perspective of a researcher, his points are equally valid in the world of software engineering.

As frameworks become ever more complex and rely upon ever more dependencies, programmers struggle to deeply understand the technology they use. Although this may appear to be a terrible situation, I suggest that it is not; if anything, it is necessary for us to progress rather than reinvent the wheel. Problems occur when the framework behaves in an unexpected manner and the deeper lack of understanding prevents us from figuring out why.

Most developers (myself included) have used the tried and true programming method: copy-paste-edit-pray-repeat. Especially prevalent among novice users without a mentor, this method is actually a fantastic way to learn. It is a perfect demonstration of the scientific method: question, hypothesize, predict, test, analyze. Well, it would be if we actually analyzed. Without the analysis, all we do is cargo cult programming.

The last time you set up a Webpack config, did you actually understand what all the options did? Or did you copy-paste-edit-pray-repeat? What about standing up your CI infrastructure? When you blindly re-run tests after they fail mysteriously, you're participating in cargo cult programming.

None of this is to say that software engineers need to understand the nuances of every transitive dependency in their project. However, copy-paste-edit-pray-repeat should always include analysis and a deeper understanding of the tools we use.
